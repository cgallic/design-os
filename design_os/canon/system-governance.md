# Canon: Design-System Governance at Scale

## Why this school is in the canon
Large design systems are the only places where design quality is enforced at industrial scale, on thousands of screens, by people who will never meet the original designer. GOV.UK, Shopify Polaris, IBM Carbon, and Google Material each solved the same problem — how to make quality *checkable* rather than dependent on taste — and they converged on the same mechanics: tiered token architecture, evidence-gated admission criteria, versioned deprecation, and automated lint. Their rules are unusually concrete (pixel values, contrast ratios, cubic-beziers, review cadences), which makes them directly enforceable by an agent harness.

## Philosophy
These systems treat design as governed infrastructure, not artwork. A component or pattern is not "good" because a senior designer likes it; it is good because it has cleared explicit, published gates. GOV.UK states the gates plainly: a proposal must be **useful** ("evidence that this component or pattern would be useful for many teams or services") and **unique** ("does not replicate something already in the Design System"); before publication it must be **usable** ("shown to work with a representative sample of users, including those with disabilities"), **consistent** (reuses existing styles, follows the style guide and coding standards), and **versatile** (tested across browsers, assistive technologies and devices). Quality is defined as evidence, and evidence is defined in advance. Anything not yet proven ships labeled "experimental" — the system is honest about its own confidence levels.

The second pillar is indirection: no raw value ever touches a component. Material 3 formalizes this as three token tiers — reference (`md.ref`, the raw palette), system (`md.sys`, roles and decisions like "primary" or "on-surface"), and component (`md.comp`, per-part slots) — with the rule that "whenever possible, component tokens should point to a system or reference token, and not contain hardcoded values such as hex codes." Polaris does the same with primitive vs. semantic tokens whose names encode intent (`color-bg-surface`, `space-card-padding`), and the W3C Design Tokens Community Group (spec 2025.10) standardized the interchange: JSON files where every token has a `$value`, a `$type` (explicit or inherited from its group), and aliases written as `{path.to.token}`. Because every visual decision flows through named tiers, the entire system can be rethemed, audited, and linted mechanically — hardcoded hex codes are not a style crime, they are a build failure.

The third pillar is that physics and evidence beat preference. Carbon derives everything from an 8px mini unit ("multiples of mini units compose the dimensions of columns, rows, boxes, along with their margins and padding") and publishes motion as six duration tokens (70–700ms) and six easing curves, with duration scaled to the size of the change. Material guarantees accessibility algorithmically: tonal palettes are generated so paired roles ("primary" / "on-primary") always meet contrast minimums, and touch targets are 48×48dp regardless of visual size. GOV.UK's design principles supply the ethos: "Start with user needs," "Design with data" ("let data drive decision-making, not hunches or guesswork"), and "Do the hard work to make it simple." Finally, nothing is immortal but nothing dies abruptly: deprecation is announced with console warnings and migration guides, deprecated assets survive at least one full major version (Carbon: deprecated in v10, present in v11, removed in v12; majors at least 12 months apart), and removal without a migration path is forbidden.

## Workflow practices
1. **Propose → review → backlog → build → gate → experimental → stable.** GOV.UK's pipeline: anyone raises a proposal on the open community backlog; the Design System team helps prepare it; it is circulated to the working group one week before the monthly meeting; the group tests it against "useful and unique"; accepted items go to the to-do backlog; before publication the implementation is checked against "usable, consistent, versatile"; most new items publish as *experimental* and lose that tag only when research proves they meet user needs.
2. **Cross-functional working group as the deciding body.** A standing group drawn from across the organization (not just the systems team) reviews contributions on a fixed cadence, so acceptance is a recorded institutional decision, not one person's taste.
3. **Evidence files travel with the component.** Screenshots of usage across services, usability findings (including disabled participants), and the browser/AT test matrix are attached to the contribution — the artifact carries its own proof.
4. **Token changes flow through the tier system.** Designers change a system/alias token to retheme globally, or a component token to change one part locally; nobody edits raw values in components. Cross-team token proposals are reviewed by product, design, and platform engineering together before release.
5. **Lint in CI, not in review meetings.** Mechanical rules (no raw hex, spacing on scale, motion tokens only) are encoded as stylelint/ESLint plugins and run on every PR; humans review only what machines cannot check.
6. **Deprecate loudly, remove slowly.** Announce in docs, warn in the console at runtime ("Deprecation: … will be removed in a future major version"), publish before/after migration examples, keep the deprecated asset alive through at least one full major version, and space majors at least 12 months apart.
7. **Design with data, iterate after live.** Decisions cite real usage of existing services; components keep being researched after publication, and the experimental tag is only removed once evidence accumulates.

## Anti-patterns (reject on sight)
- A hex code, rgb(), or raw pixel value inside a component's styles — every value must be a token reference.
- A "semantic" token named after its appearance (`color-red-500`, `blue-button`) instead of its role (`color-text-danger`, `color-bg-primary`).
- A component token that skips the tier system and hardcodes a value, breaking global retheming.
- A new component that duplicates an existing one ("we needed our own card") without evidence it is better — fails the *unique* criterion.
- Shipping a pattern as stable with no usability evidence, no disabled participants, no assistive-technology testing — at best it is *experimental* and must say so.
- Off-scale spacing: 13px padding, 22px gaps — anything not on the published scale built from the mini unit.
- Ad-hoc motion: `transition: all 0.3s linear`, durations invented per-component, entrance and exit sharing one curve, or a 1-second UI animation.
- Text placed on a colored container using anything other than the container's paired "on-" color role.
- Touch targets sized to the visual glyph (24×24) instead of the required 48×48dp hit area.
- Silent removal: deleting a component in a minor release, or in the next major with no console warning and no migration guide.
- Governance by decree: acceptance decisions made privately, off-backlog, with no recorded criteria — the review queue as a black box.
- Enforcing token rules by asking reviewers to eyeball PRs instead of encoding them as automated lint.
