# Canon: Dieter Rams & Functionalist Industrial Design

## Why this school is in the canon

Dieter Rams ran Braun's product design for four decades (design director 1962–1995, ~500 products) and co-created the Vitsoe 606 shelving system, still in production after 60+ years — the strongest empirical evidence any designer has for "long-lasting." His Ten Principles of Good Design are the most-cited quality rubric in the field, and his 1984 essay "Omit the Unimportant" gives the sharpest single test in all of design: remove everything whose removal loses nothing. The Fukasawa/Morrison "Super Normal" line extends the same functionalism to a testable preference for archetypes over novelty. This school supplies the harness's reduction, honesty, and restraint rules.

## Philosophy

Rams starts from purpose, not appearance: "Every industrial product serves a specific purpose. People do not buy a specific product just to look at it." Design's job is to make function directly legible — "items should be designed in such a way that their function and attributes are directly understood." Anything that does not serve understanding or use is noise, and noise is not neutral: "the fewer the opportunities used to create informative design, the more design serves to evoke emotional responses." Decoration is therefore not merely wasteful but actively competes with communication. The governing move is subtractive: "omit the unimportant in order to emphasize the important." Crucially, "less, but better" is not austerity for economy's sake — Rams explicitly says arriving at a convincing form "by employing simple means is surely a difficult task. The other way is easier." Simplicity is the expensive option, bought with discipline.

The second pillar is honesty and unobtrusiveness. Products are tools, "neither decorative objects nor works of art"; their design should be "neutral and restrained, to leave room for the user's self-expression." Good design "does not make a product more innovative, powerful or valuable than it really is" and "does not attempt to manipulate the consumer." Rams frames attention-grabbing design as ethical failure: "I take a stand against the ruthless exploitation of people's weaknesses for visual and haptic signals... The festival of colors and forms and the entertainment of form sensations enlarges the world's chaos." He refuses to be "dominated... excited, stupified, or amazed" by objects. Applied to digital products this bans dark patterns, fake urgency, attention-seeking motion, and any element whose primary job is stimulus rather than information.

The third pillar is rigor of execution: "consistent in every detail... Nothing must be arbitrary or left to chance. Care and accuracy in the design process show respect towards the user." At Braun this meant proportions worked out to the last millimeter, one typeface (Akzidenz-Grotesk, mostly lowercase), a neutral chassis palette with at most one or two signal colors carrying fixed meanings (on the Braun ET calculator: input functions green, output functions red), and rarely-used controls physically demoted to the back of the device. Fukasawa and Morrison's Super Normal adds the archetype test: the familiar, "normal" form is usually more useful than the special one, so novelty must be justified by a real change in function or technology — "innovative design always develops in tandem with innovative technology, and can never be an end in itself."

## Workflow practices

1. Design sits inside the company, next to engineering, from day one. Braun's design department (est. 1956 under Fritz Eichler) worked in-house with engineers; Rams: early problems came from "insufficient cooperation between designers and engineers... we first had to explore and develop the types and means of cooperation." Design is never applied after the fact.
2. Everything is clarified in advance with models and prototypes. "In industrial design, everything for the production has to be clarified in advance with models and prototypes, all the details... Otherwise you don't proceed to the production stage." Review physical/interactive artifacts, not descriptions.
3. Maintain one design program, not individual icons. The achievement Eichler, Rams and Weiss are credited with is "establishing and then maintaining the unity of this program" across the whole product line — every new product is reviewed against the existing system for consistency of typography, color semantics, proportions, and controls.
4. Work the details to the limit: proportions to the last millimeter, details to the last screw fastener. Detail review is a distinct, mandatory pass, and "care and accuracy in the design process show respect towards the user."
5. Reduce iteratively and deliberately: each review pass asks what can be omitted; elements earn their place by carrying information or function. "Arriving at a really convincing, harmonious form by employing simple means is surely a difficult task" — budget real time for reduction, it is not the cheap path.
6. Question everything, including the brief: refuse "to submit to widespread demands or structures of the market without asking for the product's use." The first review question is always "what is this for, and who uses it how often?"
7. Interdisciplinary sign-off: design, technology, and communication (advertising/marketing at Braun) are aligned so the product, its interface, and its presentation tell one honest story.
8. Longevity check: before shipping, ask whether the design will still be right in ten years — deliberately strip devices that are only fashionable now (the Vitsoe 606 has needed only minor changes since 1960).

## Anti-patterns (reject on sight)

- Decorative accent colors — any hue applied "to look lively" rather than to signal a control, state, or category.
- Two colors meaning the same thing, or one color meaning two things, anywhere in the product.
- Multiple typefaces, display/novelty fonts, or type set for expression rather than reading.
- Attention-seeking motion: looping animations, pulsing badges, blinking elements, autoplaying movement not tied to a process state.
- Dark patterns of any kind: fake scarcity/urgency countdowns, confirm-shaming, manipulated consent defaults, progress bars that lie — design that "attempts to manipulate the consumer."
- Design riddles: mystery-meat navigation, unlabeled icon-only controls for primary actions, products that need a tutorial to reveal their basic purpose.
- Rarely-used controls given prime placement (settings gear in the hero, admin actions in the main toolbar).
- Novel interaction patterns adopted for differentiation where a familiar archetype exists and function is unchanged (the failure Super Normal names: "special is generally less useful than normal").
- Arbitrary values: one-off spacing, off-grid alignment, near-miss sizes — anything "left to chance."
- Trend-chasing devices justified only by fashion (the current year's gloss/blur/gradient fad applied without functional reason) — "modish sensation" that "outdates products quickly."
- Skeuomorphic or stylistic flourishes that promise affordances that don't exist (honesty violation).
- The "festival of colors and forms": screens designed to be maximally recognized rather than maximally understood — aggressive signal-stacking to win attention.

## In their words

- "Less, but better — because it concentrates on the essential aspects, and the products are not burdened with non-essentials. Back to purity, back to simplicity." — Dieter Rams, Ten Principles (principle 10, Vitsoe)
- "One of the most significant design principles is to omit the unimportant in order to emphasize the important." — Dieter Rams, "Omit the Unimportant," Design Issues 1, no. 1 (1984)
- "Good design means as little design as possible. Not for reasons of economy or convenience. Arriving at a really convincing, harmonious form by employing simple means is surely a difficult task. The other way is easier and... more thoughtless with respect to production." — Rams (1984)
- "Complicated, unnecessary forms are nothing more than designers' escapades that function as self-expression instead of expressing the product's functions." — Rams (1984)
- "Items should be designed in such a way that their function and attributes are directly understood... Design riddles are impudent and products that are informative, understandable, and clear are pleasant and agreeable." — Rams (1984)
- "I don't want to be dominated, nor do I want to be excited, stupified, or amazed... The choices are sensible: disciplined simplicity or forced, oppressive, stupifying expression. For me there is only one way: discipline." — Rams (1984)
- "I take a stand against the ruthless exploitation of people's weaknesses for visual and haptic signals... The festival of colors and forms and the entertainment of form sensations enlarges the world's chaos." — Rams (1984)
- "Good design is honest. It does not make a product more innovative, powerful or valuable than it really is. It does not attempt to manipulate the consumer with promises that cannot be kept." — Rams, Ten Principles (principle 6, Vitsoe)
- "Good design is thorough down to the last detail. Nothing must be arbitrary or left to chance. Care and accuracy in the design process show respect towards the user." — Rams, Ten Principles (principle 8, Vitsoe)
- "Products fulfilling a purpose are like tools. They are neither decorative objects nor works of art. Their design should therefore be both neutral and restrained, to leave room for the user's self-expression." — Rams, Ten Principles (principle 5, Vitsoe)
- "Innovative design always develops in tandem with innovative technology, and can never be an end in itself." — Rams, Ten Principles (principle 1, Vitsoe)
- "Special is generally less useful than normal." — Jasper Morrison, Super Normal: Sensations of the Ordinary (2006)
- "Order rather than confusion, quiet rather than loud, unobtrusive rather than exciting, sparse rather than profuse, and well-balanced rather than exalted." — Rudolf Schonwandt, describing Rams's philosophy
- Caption evidence of operational rules, Rams (1984): "Input functions of this Braun pocket calculator are coded in green, output functions are in red." / "The operating components that are seldom used are set in the back of the stereo."
