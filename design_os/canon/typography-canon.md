# Canon: Typography Canon — Butterick, Bringhurst & Lupton

## Why this school is in the canon

These three sources are the modern English-language canon of typographic rules, and — unusually for design writing — they publish actual numbers. Matthew Butterick's *Practical Typography* (2013–, practicaltypography.com) distills professional typesetting into a checkable "summary of key rules" with explicit thresholds (point sizes, 120–145% line spacing, 45–90 character lines, 5–12% letterspacing for caps). Robert Bringhurst's *The Elements of Typographic Style* (1992, 4th ed. 2012) is the field's reference bible — the source of the 45–75-character measure, the M/4 word space, baseline-multiple vertical rhythm, and the modular-scale doctrine. Ellen Lupton's *Thinking with Type* (2004, 3rd ed. 2024) supplies the enumerable blacklist: her illustrated "type crimes" (faux styles, stretched glyphs, tracked lowercase, tentative size differences) convert directly into automated lint rules for text-heavy artifacts.

## Philosophy

The unifying claim is that typography is functional, not decorative, and its function is to serve the reader's attention. Butterick: "Typography matters because it helps conserve the most valuable resource you have as a writer — reader attention." His test of quality is explicitly utilitarian: good typography "reinforces the meaning of the text," and "typography that reinforces the meaning of the text, even if aesthetically unpleasant, is a success. Sometimes ugly is better than pretty." Bringhurst states the same ethic as a vocation: "Typography exists to honor content." Type should "draw attention before being read, then relinquish that attention" — it invites the reader in, then disappears. Lupton compresses it to a definition: "Typography is what language looks like." All three therefore reject taste as the arbiter; a typographic choice is judged by whether it transmits the text's goals, which means the designer must actually know those goals before choosing anything ("read the text before designing it" is Bringhurst's first instruction).

The second shared conviction is that body text is the unit of work, and that it is governed by a small number of interlocking quantities. Butterick's opening move — before fonts, before decoration — is the "big four": point size, line spacing, line length, and font, because "there's more body text than anything else," and he attaches a range to each. Bringhurst deepens the same variables into a system of proportion: line length maps to the span of the eye (45–75 characters, 66 ideal); word space is a rhythmic unit near a quarter em; leading is a basic beat and "the total amount of vertical space consumed by each departure from the main text should be an even multiple of the basic leading." His most quoted analogy makes proportion the whole game: "Space in typography is like time in music. It is infinitely divisible, but a few proportional intervals can be much more useful than a limitless choice of arbitrary quantities" — hence type sizes chosen from a modular scale, not invented per element. Lupton extends the spatial argument: "Design is as much an act of spacing as an act of marking" — the gaps between and around letters are design material equal to the letters themselves.

Third: honesty of material and restraint of signal. All three treat mechanically faked typography as fraud — Lupton's type-crime list is a catalog of fakes (pseudo-italics "look forced and unnatural," pseudo small caps, horizontally or vertically scaled letterforms that distort stroke contrast) and of over-signaling (hierarchies that shout with size + bold + color at once, size steps so small they "look tentative and arbitrary"). Butterick's version is the emphasis budget: bold *or* italic, never both, "with restraint"; underlining never; all-caps only below one line; one exclamation point per three pages. Bringhurst's version is the sheep line he made famous (quoting Frederic Goudy): "A man who would letterspace lower case would steal sheep." Restraint is not minimal-ism for its own sake — it is what keeps the few remaining signals legible as signals.
