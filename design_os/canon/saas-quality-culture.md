# Canon: Linear, Stripe & Vercel — How Elite SaaS Teams Systematize Design Quality

## Why this school is in the canon

Linear, Stripe, and Vercel are the three companies most often cited as proof that software quality can be *institutionalized* rather than depend on a lone genius: Linear made "quality as the founding strategy" a business result (Karri Saarinen: "Product quality is the ultimate moat for a business"), Stripe turned craft into measurable operations under Katie Dill (friction logs, "walk the store," scored essential journeys, quarterly quality reviews — with quantified ROI: +20% conversion from an email redesign, +11.9% revenue from Optimized Checkout), and Vercel went furthest toward this harness's own goal — literally encoding design judgment as linters and evidence-backed review rules for AI agents. Where other canon schools supply aesthetic rules, this school supplies the *process* layer: how work gets reviewed, what a quality bar actually checks, and who is accountable for holding it.

## Philosophy

The unifying belief is that quality is not a final polish step but a *system property of the team and its habits*. Saarinen defines quality as craft: "the deliberate attention put into making something excellent, not because someone is checking, but because it matters to the maker" — and argues that every technology cycle (mass production, cloud, now AI) tempts teams to trade judgment for speed and metrics ("the industry shifted from asking 'Does this feel right?' to 'Does it convert?'"). Stripe's operating principle makes the same claim morally: "Well-crafted work indicates care for the user, and beautiful work indicates care for the world." The corollary — often compressed to "beauty is a lagging indicator of diligence" — is that surface polish cannot be faked at the end; it is the visible residue of a diligent process. Dill's framing separates the two: "Craft is about the how — the thinking, work, and mastery... quality is the output. You can have craft without quality, but rarely quality without craft."

Structurally, all three reject the handoff model. Linear's rule is explicit: "There's no 'handoff to dev.' You're never off the hook" — small connected teams (3–5 people) iterate the *built* product toward "right," and the spec is "your baseline minimum viable product, not your goal." Quality is protected not by perfectionism but by scope control ("The simplest way to increase quality is to reduce scope") and by a hard ship gate: work may start rough, but "don't show it to customers before it passes your quality bar." Stripe operationalizes the same instinct at scale: everyone (all new employees are trained on it) writes friction logs while using the product as a real user would; leadership "walks the store" through the top ~15 essential journeys on a quarterly cadence and scores them on a deliberately coarse five-point color scale ("avoid false precision"); quality reviews (PQR/QBRs) sit alongside business reviews so quality has the same institutional standing as revenue. Dill's bar is three-layered and must be assessed in order: utility (does it solve the problem?), usability (can people access the value?), beauty (is the execution refined and enjoyable?) — "we must get all three right," and "utility before beauty" must never become an excuse to skip beauty, because beauty *is* functional: "When something is more attractive, compelling, and clear, people tend to gravitate towards it."

On measurement, the school is deliberately two-handed. Linear distrusts dashboards for design decisions — "Data can be a crutch... you can't expect data to tell you how" — and validates quality through dogfooding, small user cohorts, and unsolicited praise ("If people are not raving about your product, you probably don't have a good product"). Stripe measures outcomes (journey scores, CSAT, support tickets, bugs) but keeps the judgment human. Vercel closes the loop for the AI age: taste is converted into evidence — "Use evidence, not taste: trace decisions to verified behavior and guidance, not preference" — deterministic rules become linters (4px spacing grid, no nested modals, accessible names on icon buttons, token-based focus rings), every reachable state (loading, empty, error, permission) must be designed, and no one — human or agent — may claim visual verification without looking at the rendered surface. The school's message to a design harness is direct: encode what is checkable, ritualize what is not, and never let speed silently lower the bar.

## Workflow practices

1. **Leadership sets the quality mandate explicitly.** Craft is declared the top priority from the top, with a business rationale, creating a "permission structure" for everyone below to spend time on quality (Saarinen, rule 1). At Stripe this is a written operating principle ("Create with craft and beauty").
2. **No handoff, ever.** Product, design, and engineering share one team and one accountability; designers stay through implementation and engineers own feel. The whole team iterates the built artifact toward "right" (Linear).
3. **Dogfood first, cohort second, public last.** Linear's gate: use it internally until it passes the bar, then invite 5–10 motivated users, watch whether they keep using it daily, fix what they hit, then widen. Stripe mirrors this with user preview programs and iteration cycles sometimes under 24 hours.
4. **Friction logs as a universal ritual.** Any Stripe employee using a product writes a friction log (context, pros/cons, stream-of-consciousness with screenshots, S/M/L size tag), shares it company-wide, and personally follows up until items land in the backlog and ship.
5. **Walk the store on a cadence.** Stripe leadership and multidisciplinary teams re-walk the top ~15 essential journeys quarterly, end to end across surfaces, scoring each on a coarse red→green 5-point scale ("avoid false precision") and tracking scores over time; complemented by CSAT, support tickets, and production-bug counts in quarterly quality business reviews (PQR/QBRs).
6. **Three-level quality bar in every review.** Utility, then usability, then beauty — all three assessed, none skipped (Dill).
7. **Cycles keep momentum without deadlines-over-quality.** Linear works in short fixed cycles, scopes projects down into shippable increments, writes issues (not user stories), and "launches and keeps launching" — quality is refined continuously rather than gated on a big-bang release.
8. **Zero-bug hygiene.** Known defects in shipped surfaces are fixed within days (Linear: 7-day policy), because tolerated bugs recalibrate the whole team's bar downward.
9. **Encode judgment as tooling.** Vercel's model: an agent-readable skill for product context, deterministic linters for everything computable (spacing grid, nested modals, a11y names, token discipline), and a review loop that mines Slack/Figma/GitHub discussions to turn recurring judgments into new written guidelines. Evals score rule-correctness separately from similarity-to-shipped-code, so flawed shipped patterns don't become the standard.
10. **Findings are evidence-backed and prioritized.** Every review finding names the location, the canonical source violated, the user consequence, and a concrete fix, prioritized P0–P3 by user impact — "use evidence, not taste" (Vercel).
11. **Hire for demonstrated craft and care**, not just skill; Linear and Figma both treat intrinsic care as unfakeable — "If you need an OKR to convince someone to care about quality, you probably have the wrong team" (Yamashita).

## Anti-patterns (reject on sight)

- **"Handoff to dev"** — a designer's mock thrown over the wall, with fidelity loss treated as engineering's problem. ("You're never off the hook.")
- **Shipping the spec verbatim** and calling it done — the spec is the floor; zero beyond-spec refinement signals no one cared during building.
- **Showing customers unfinished work** because the deadline arrived — cut scope instead; the quality bar does not flex.
- **A "utility first" roadmap where beauty never arrives** — the perpetual "we'll polish later" backlog item (Dill explicitly calls this failure mode out).
- **Success-state-only builds** — views with no designed loading, empty, error, or permission states.
- **Metric-worship for design decisions** — A/B-testing your way to a design, "does it convert?" replacing "does this feel right?"; data as crutch.
- **False-precision quality scores** — 100-point quality indexes; Stripe deliberately uses a 5-color scale.
- **Preference toggles instead of decisions** — shipping a setting because the team couldn't commit to an opinionated default.
- **Off-grid magic numbers** — arbitrary 13px paddings, one-off shadows, className overrides of system component color/radius, custom focus rings.
- **Nested modals**, icon buttons without accessible names, selects hiding 2–3 static options.
- **"Verified" from code reading alone** — sign-off without ever looking at the rendered surface.
- **Big permanent feature teams** — headcount as the answer to quality ("the more people... the more opinions and deliberation, which dilutes the quality of the execution").
- **Tolerated known bugs** — defects aging in the backlog past a week, silently lowering the whole team's bar.
- **Taste claims without evidence in review** — "I don't like it" findings with no location, source, consequence, or fix.
